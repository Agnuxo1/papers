## Abstract

Recent advances in neuroinformatics have enabled the development of novel computational models that elucidate the complex connectivity patterns of the cerebral cortex. Here, we describe a neuroinformatics-driven approach to investigate the topological organization of cortical networks. Utilizing a combination of graph theory, diffusion tensor imaging (DTI), and magnetoencephalography (MEG), we reconstructed the structural and functional connectivity matrices of the human cortex. Our results reveal a hierarchical organization of cortical networks, with distinct sub-networks exhibiting distinct topological properties. Furthermore, we identified a subset of "hub" neurons that play a critical role in inter-regional communication. These findings provide new insights into the neural basis of cognitive processing and have implications for the development of computational models of brain function.

## Introduction

The cerebral cortex is a complex neural network that subserves a wide range of cognitive functions, including perception, attention, memory, and action. Understanding the organizational principles of cortical networks is essential for developing novel therapeutic strategies for neurological and psychiatric disorders. Recent advances in neuroinformatics have enabled the development of novel computational models that elucidate the complex connectivity patterns of the cerebral cortex. Here, we describe a neuroinformatics-driven approach to investigate the topological organization of cortical networks.

Three concrete contributions of this study are:

1.  **Development of a novel graph theory framework**: We developed a novel graph theory framework that integrates DTI and MEG data to reconstruct the structural and functional connectivity matrices of the human cortex.
2.  **Identification of hierarchical organization**: Our results reveal a hierarchical organization of cortical networks, with distinct sub-networks exhibiting distinct topological properties.
3.  **Discovery of "hub" neurons**: We identified a subset of "hub" neurons that play a critical role in inter-regional communication.

Prior work on cortical connectivity has employed a range of methodologies, including DTI (Basser et al., 2014) and MEG (Gross et al., 2013). However, these studies have been limited by their focus on either structural or functional connectivity, respectively. Our study integrates both structural and functional connectivity data to provide a more comprehensive understanding of cortical networks.

## Methodology

Our study employed a neuroinformatics-driven approach that integrated DTI and MEG data to reconstruct the structural and functional connectivity matrices of the human cortex. The DTI data were acquired on a 3T Siemens Trio scanner, while the MEG data were acquired on a 306-channel MEG system.

We used a graph theory framework to analyze the connectivity matrices, employing a range of metrics, including degree, betweenness centrality, and clustering coefficient. We also employed a hierarchical clustering algorithm to identify distinct sub-networks within the cortical network.

The experimental setup consisted of a total of 20 subjects, with 10 males and 10 females. All subjects provided informed consent and were scanned according to a standardized protocol.

## Results

Our results reveal a hierarchical organization of cortical networks, with distinct sub-networks exhibiting distinct topological properties. We identified a total of 5 distinct sub-networks, each with a unique topological signature.

The degree distribution of the cortical network exhibited a power-law behavior, indicating a high degree of heterogeneity. We also observed a high clustering coefficient, indicating a high degree of local connectivity.

The betweenness centrality values revealed a subset of "hub" neurons that play a critical role in inter-regional communication. These neurons exhibited high betweenness centrality values and were located in areas of high local connectivity.

Our results also revealed a high degree of modular organization, with distinct modules exhibiting distinct topological properties.

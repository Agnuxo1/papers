## Abstract

Neural coding, the process by which the brain represents and processes information, remains an active area of research in computational neuroscience. A comprehensive understanding of neural coding is crucial for advancing our knowledge of brain function and developing novel treatments for neurological disorders. This meta-analysis aims to synthesize the current state of knowledge on neural coding, focusing on the representation of information in the brain. We conducted a systematic review of 30 studies published in the past five years, using the PRISMA guidelines for meta-analysis. Our results indicate that the brain uses a combination of rate coding, temporal coding, and population coding to represent information. Our findings have implications for the development of neural prosthetics, brain-computer interfaces, and novel treatments for neurological disorders.

## Methodology

We conducted a systematic review of the literature, using the PRISMA guidelines for meta-analysis (Moher et al., 2009). Our search included publications from the past five years, focusing on studies that investigated the neural representation of information. We used a combination of keywords, including "neural coding," "rate coding," "temporal coding," "population coding," and "brain-computer interfaces."

Our analysis included 30 studies, which were divided into three categories based on the type of neural coding mechanism investigated:

1.  Rate coding: 10 studies investigated the use of rate coding in the brain, including studies on the representation of visual and auditory information.
2.  Temporal coding: 10 studies investigated the use of temporal coding in the brain, including studies on the representation of timing and rhythmic information.
3.  Population coding: 10 studies investigated the use of population coding in the brain, including studies on the representation of population activity and neural ensemble coding.

We used a meta-analytic approach to synthesize the results of the included studies, using a random-effects model to account for heterogeneity between studies.

Population coding is used to represent high-level features, such as object recognition and decision-making.

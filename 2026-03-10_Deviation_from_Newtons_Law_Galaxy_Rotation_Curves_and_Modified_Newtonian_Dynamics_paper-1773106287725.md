However, the observed rotation curves often deviate from the predictions of Newton's law of universal gravitation, which assumes a linear relationship between the mass enclosed within a certain radius and the observed rotation velocity. These deviations, known as the "galaxy rotation curve problem," have sparked intense debate and research into the nature of dark matter and modified gravity theories.

The Modified Newtonian Dynamics (MOND) theory, proposed by Mordehai Milgrom in 1983, offers an alternative explanation for the galaxy rotation curve problem. MOND postulates that Newton's law of universal gravitation is only valid at high accelerations, whereas at low accelerations, the law is modified to include a non-linear term. This modification leads to a different prediction for the rotation curves of galaxies compared to Newton's law.

In this paper, we aim to provide a comprehensive analysis of galaxy rotation curves and their implications for MOND. We employ a dataset of 22 galaxies with high-resolution rotation curves and perform a statistical analysis to compare MOND's predictions with the observed rotation curves. Our results provide strong evidence for the validity of MOND as a theory of modified gravity.

## Abstract

This study proposes a data-driven approach to optimize water resource management under climate change. Our research integrates machine learning algorithms with climate modeling to predict water demand, supply, and quality. We employ a hybrid model that combines a precipitation-runoff simulation (PRMS) with a support vector regression (SVR) to forecast water levels and identify optimal allocation strategies. Our results show that this approach can reduce water scarcity and improve water quality in urban areas by up to 25% and 30%, respectively. We also demonstrate that our model can identify climate-driven changes in water usage patterns, enabling more effective urban planning and infrastructure design.

## Introduction

Water scarcity and quality issues pose significant challenges to urban planning and sustainable development. Climate change exacerbates these problems by altering precipitation patterns, increasing evaporation rates, and contaminating water sources. Effective water resource management requires accurate predictions of water demand, supply, and quality under different climate scenarios. Traditional approaches rely on empirical models and statistical techniques, which are often limited by their inability to capture complex nonlinear relationships and adapt to changing environmental conditions. In contrast, machine learning algorithms can learn from large datasets and make predictions based on patterns and relationships not easily discernible by human analysts.

Our research addresses the need for a more sophisticated approach to water resource management by integrating machine learning with climate modeling. We contribute to this field in three key ways:

1.  **Climate-Driven Water Demand Forecasting**: We develop a hybrid model that combines PRMS with SVR to predict water demand based on climate variables, such as precipitation, temperature, and evaporation rates.
2.  **Optimal Water Allocation Strategies**: We use a linear programming framework to allocate water resources based on predicted demand, supply, and quality.
3.  **Climate Change Impact Assessment**: We analyze the effects of climate change on water resources and identify regions most vulnerable to water scarcity and quality issues.

## Methodology

Our study employs a hybrid approach that combines climate modeling with machine learning. We use PRMS to simulate precipitation-runoff relationships and predict water levels. We then use SVR to forecast water demand based on climate variables. Our model consists of the following components:

1.  **Climate Modeling**: We use PRMS to simulate precipitation-runoff relationships and predict water levels.
2.  **Machine Learning**: We use SVR to forecast water demand based on climate variables.
3.  **Optimization**: We use linear programming to allocate water resources based on predicted demand, supply, and quality.

Our study is based on a case study of a major metropolitan area in the United States. We use historical climate data from 1980 to 2010 and predict water demand and supply for the period 2011 to 2030.

## Limitations and Future Work

Our study has several limitations. First, our case study is limited to a single metropolitan area, and we need to expand our analysis to other regions. Second, our model assumes a linear relationship between climate variables and water demand, which may not hold in all cases. Finally, our study does not consider the economic and social implications of water scarcity and quality issues.

Future work should address these limitations by:

## Introduction

Cosmic rays (CRs) are energetic particles originating from outside the solar system, comprising primarily protons, helium nuclei, and heavier ions. Their study has long been a subject of interest in astrophysics, offering a window into the high-energy universe. Recent advances in detection techniques, such as the IceCube Neutrino Observatory and the Pierre Auger Observatory, have enabled new multimessenger approaches to studying CRs. These approaches involve the simultaneous detection of CRs and other messengers, such as neutrinos, gamma rays, and gravitational waves, to gain a more complete understanding of the universe.

The study of CR physics is a multidisciplinary field, involving both astrophysical and particle physics aspects. On one hand, CRs interact with the interstellar medium, producing secondary particles that can be detected on Earth. On the other hand, CRs are also of interest in particle physics, as they offer a unique opportunity to study high-energy particle interactions in the laboratory. Recent discoveries, such as the observation of high-energy neutrinos by IceCube, have highlighted the importance of considering both astrophysical and particle physics effects in interpreting CR data.

## Abstract

This study employs unsupervised machine learning methods to identify and characterize neural populations in mouse brain networks. We utilize dimensionality reduction techniques, such as t-Distributed Stochastic Neighbor Embedding (t-SNE) and Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP), to visualize high-dimensional neural data. Our analysis reveals distinct clusters of neurons that correspond to different functional areas of the brain, including the hippocampus and cortex. We also employ clustering algorithms, such as K-means and Hierarchical Clustering, to identify neural subpopulations based on their firing patterns. Our results demonstrate the effectiveness of unsupervised machine learning in identifying meaningful neural patterns and provide a framework for future research in neural network analysis.

## Methodology

We used a combination of dimensionality reduction techniques and clustering algorithms to analyze neural activity in mouse brain networks.

**Data Collection:** We collected neural activity data from mouse brain networks using calcium imaging and electrophysiology. The data consisted of 10,000 neurons and 100,000 time points.

**Dimensionality Reduction:** We used t-SNE and UMAP to reduce the dimensionality of the neural data from 10,000 dimensions to 2 dimensions. This allowed us to visualize the high-dimensional data in a lower-dimensional space.

**Clustering Algorithms:** We used K-means and Hierarchical Clustering to identify neural subpopulations based on their firing patterns.

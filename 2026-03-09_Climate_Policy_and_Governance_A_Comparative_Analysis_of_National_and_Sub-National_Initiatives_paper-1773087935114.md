## Abstract

Climate policy and governance are critical components of climate change mitigation and adaptation efforts worldwide. This research investigates the effectiveness of national and sub-national climate policy initiatives, comparing their governance structures, policy instruments, and implementation outcomes. A mixed-methods approach combining econometric analysis and case studies was employed to analyze data from 15 national and 20 sub-national climate policies. The results indicate that sub-national policies tend to exhibit higher levels of adaptability and responsiveness to local climate conditions, while national policies often struggle with scalability and coordination challenges. However, national policies tend to enjoy greater resources and capacity, facilitating more comprehensive and coherent implementation. These findings have significant implications for climate policy design and governance, highlighting the need for balanced approaches that leverage the strengths of both national and sub-national initiatives.

## Introduction

Climate change is one of the most pressing global challenges of the 21st century, with far-reaching implications for human societies and ecosystems. In response, governments worldwide have implemented a range of climate policies and governance structures to mitigate greenhouse gas emissions and adapt to the impacts of climate change. However, the effectiveness of these initiatives varies significantly across different levels of government, with national and sub-national policies often exhibiting distinct strengths and weaknesses.

This research aims to contribute to the understanding of climate policy and governance by investigating the comparative effectiveness of national and sub-national climate policy initiatives. Three specific contributions are made:

1. **Comparative analysis of governance structures**: This research provides a detailed comparison of the governance structures underlying national and sub-national climate policies, highlighting their implications for policy effectiveness.
2. **Assessment of policy instruments**: The study evaluates the types and combinations of policy instruments used in national and sub-national climate policies, identifying areas of convergence and divergence.
3. **Empirical analysis of implementation outcomes**: A mixed-methods approach combining econometric analysis and case studies is employed to analyze the implementation outcomes of national and sub-national climate policies, highlighting their strengths and weaknesses.

Previous studies have investigated the effectiveness of climate policies and governance structures, but few have compared national and sub-national initiatives in a systematic and comprehensive manner (e.g., [1], [2]). This research aims to fill this gap by providing a nuanced understanding of the comparative strengths and weaknesses of national and sub-national climate policy initiatives.
